About

I am a PhD candidate in philosophy at the University of Hamburg, supervised by Prof. Dr. Rolf W. Puster. My dissertation, Agency and Legitimate Governance: From Agency to the Limits to Government Intervention, develops a theory of political legitimacy grounded in action theory, asking where and why government intervention is justified.

My work sits at the intersection of political philosophy, Austrian school economics, and the history of political economy. I am particularly interested in the epistemic and normative dimensions of economic policy, theories of coercion, and the classical liberal tradition.

I am currently a Carl Menger Fellow at the Mercatus Center (2025–2026) and an EPICENTER Fellow (since 2025), and hold a competitive doctoral scholarship from the Friedrich Naumann Foundation.

Research

My research addresses three overlapping areas:

  • Limits to Government Intervention: Drawing on Mises's interventionism thesis and the epistemics of liberal transition, I ask when and why state interference in voluntary exchange is illegitimate.
  • Political Legitimacy & Action Theory: Starting from a theory of agency, I develop criteria for what counts as coercion, manipulation, and legitimate governance.
  • History of Political Economy: I work on Hayek, Mises, ordoliberalism, and the relationship between economic systems and political regimes (the "roads to serfdom" thesis).

Current working papers include "Idealisations and Abstractions" (accepted with minor revisions, Journal for General Philosophy of Science), "Short-term and Long-term Stability" (under peer-review), and "A Concept in Need of Clarification: Authoritarian Liberalism."
